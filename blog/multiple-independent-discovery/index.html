Multiple Independent Discovery

By Yann Hulot April 10, 2016 Comment Tweet Like +1

Picture this, you are in the shower scrubbing yourself and all of a sudden you have a flash, like a revelation. You just had a great idea or you think you did anyway! You dry yourself up quickly, get your laptop and start googling frenetically. Much to your dismay, you discover that they are not only one but at least 5 companies that have turned your idea into a product and are actively selling it.

Your dreams are shattered and it takes a while for you to go back to what you were doing before that idea came to you. As Tupac said: Life Goes On.

You have probably been there and so have I. It is called Multiple independent discovery. Given the size of the Earth’s population, one can only assume that many people are going to come up with the same solution to a given problem.

If we look at businesses, we can see that the same principle apply. What makes a Samsung Galaxy S6 Plus different from an Iphone S6 Plus? They both solve the same basic problem, communication. The key there is the execution.

When I first arrived in Australia, I came up with the idea to create a job board that I thought could change completely how casual workers would find jobs. At the time, I was right. There were few competitors besides Gumtree and OneShift.

Fast forward 2 years, and competitors have popped up everywhere. What happened? The answer is again: Multiple Independent discovery.

JobCompanion had many flaws but the one that ultimately led me to shut it down was that it took roughly 2 years to become operational. The reason for such a long development time is due to outside circumstances and also due to my own shortcomings.

Even though that project failed, I learned a lot of lessons along the way. The first one being: Shipping a half-finished product in a short amount of time is better than no product at all.

Lesson number 2: It’s fine if the idea you had for a product or a service is not truly original as long as it is better than the product or service of your competitors.

Lesson number 3: Execution is key, ideas are great but virtually worthless. If you have an idea, act on it, make a sale, showcase it, make it real. Only then will you know if it is viable. If it is only in your head then it may as well not exist.

As a final word, I would like to stress out that failure is normal. Most business tycoons, artists and world leaders have failed at some point in their lives. In fact I will go as far as saying that failure is entirely part of success. So, don’t be afraid! Build something worthwhile and have some fun while doing it.
